Sexual Self

This chapter will discuss the concept of sexual self. It highlights biological and environmental factors that shape sexual development. It will provide you with suggestions on how to begin the process of redefining your sexual self.

What is Sexual Self?



Sexual self refers to how one thinks about themself as a sexual individual. It speaks of your sexual health, sexual orientation, gender identity, and expression and values around your sexuality. The sexuality or sex of a person is classified as male, female or intersex. A person typically has their sex assigned at birth based on physiological characteristics including their genitalia and chromosome compositions. Gender is a term that refers to social or cultural distinctions associated with a given sex. Gender identity is what is inside us; how we feel about our own gender. Sexuality is about who you are attracted to, sexually or romantically. This is also called your sexual orientation where one’s physical attraction and emotional attraction overlap.


In the present study, factors affecting sexual self-concept were categorized to the following:

  • Biological Factors
  • Psychological Factors
  • Social Factors




Biological Factors


Sexual self-concept is feelings, beliefs, and perceptions that people have about their sexual relationships that accordingly adjust their behaviors. Adolescence is the time when the individual's understandings and perceptions of who they are as a sexual creature or their sexual self-concept are stabilized and improved many months or even years before the beginning of any romantic relationship can occur. The aspects of sexual self-concept become apparent in early adolescence.




Psychological Factors


The impact of body image is the attitude and perception of the individual and others regarding his/her own appearance and the body. Body image is a multifaceted structure that combines individuals’ understandings and attitudes about the body, especially the physical appearance. This includes knowledge related to appearance (schemas), feelings and behaviors. The evaluation of body image can affect interests and experiences of the person during sexual activities. It seems that sexual self-concept is affected by women’s self-perceptions.


The history of sexual abuse in childhood may affect the development of positive sexual self-concept and put individuals at high risk for sexual dysfunction. Women with a history of child sexual abuse have a lower sexual self-concept compared with else. Moreover, women with sexual disorders have a weaker positive self-concept and stronger negative self-concept compared with those without sexual dysfunctions. Therefore, it is likely that sexual self-concept plays a role in the sexual function of abused women in childhood. Sexual interactions with coercion and abuse can cause negative emotions such as guilt, shame, anger, sorrow and frustration. The survivors of sexual abuse in childhood appear less romantic, sentimental or sensual compared with other women.


Mental health can affect sexual self-concept, resulting in more negative feelings of sexual inadequacy in schizophrenia and depression patients. They also report less sexual satisfaction. The fear of incompetence and negative emotions in patients with schizophrenia is higher resulting in considerable distress when trying to have sexual relationships. Patients with depressive disorders are unable to enjoy their lives and gradually lose interest in daily activities. Sexual self-concept in patients with depression is changed due to notable volition-drive deficit, low self-esteem, feelings of sexual inadequacy and worthlessness.




Social Factors


The role of Parents and peers; Parents are usually the first educators of the sexual identity of children and teach them about love, kindness and humility. Students also report that most information on sexuality is learned by their peers. Furthermore, the socialization of sexual identity means providing information, attitudes and values about sexual issues. This is a complex and multidimensional process that occurs gradually over years using various sources. It encompasses different topics including the biology of reproduction, values and attitudes about dating, sexual relationships, love, emotional and romantic relationships. Sexual messages are transmitted through verbal, non-verbal, direct and indirect methods, and are often subtle, ambiguous and contradictory.





Challenges of Sexual Choices

  • Sexual assault and harassment
    • - Unfortunately, college campuses are not immune to sexual harrassment. Student may experience or witness such incidents that can be found impact on their sexual and overall well-being.
  • Body Image Concerns
    • - Some students may face body image concerns, comparing themselves to societal standards or feeling insecure about their physical appearance.
  • Sexual Identity and Preferences
    • - College is a time when many students begin to explore their sexual identity and preferences. This can be a challenging and confusing process as they navigate their feelings and understand their own desires.
  • No Comprehensive Sexual Education
    • - Many students enter college without comprehensive sexual education. This lack of knowledge about sexual health, consent, contraception, and sexually transmitted infections (STIs) can lead to confusion and potential risks.




Contributions of Sexual Self to the Understanding of the Self


The sexual self is an essential aspect of an individual's identity and contributes significantly to their overall understanding of self. It encompasses a person's sexual orientation, desires, fantasies, behaviors, and attitudes toward their own sexuality.


Here are several ways in which the sexual self contributes to the overall understanding of the self:

  • Identity Formation
    • - Sexual orientation and gender identity are fundamental components of one's identity. Understanding and accepting one's sexual orientation or gender identity can contribute to a more holistic understanding of oneself. It allows individuals to explore and define their place within society and form connections with others who share similar identities.
  • Personal Exploration
    • - The sexual self provides a platform for personal exploration and self-discovery. By exploring one's sexual desires, fantasies, and preferences, individuals can gain insights into their own needs, boundaries, and what brings them pleasure. This exploration fosters a deeper understanding of oneself, leading to personal growth and a sense of authenticity.
  • Emotional and Physical Well-being
    • - The sexual self plays a crucial role in an individual's emotional and physical well-being. Understanding one's sexual needs, boundaries, and desires helps promote a healthy and fulfilling sexual life. It allows individuals to engage in sexual experiences that are aligned with their values, preferences, and consensual boundaries, leading to greater satisfaction and overall well-being.
  • Relationship Dynamics
    • - The sexual self influences the dynamics within intimate relationships. Understanding one's sexual needs and communicating them effectively with a partner fosters healthier and more satisfying relationships. It helps individuals establish compatibility, navigate consent, and negotiate desires within the context of a partnership. A deeper understanding of one's sexual self can promote empathy, intimacy, and mutual respect between partners.
  • Social and Cultural Context
    • - The sexual self is shaped by social and cultural factors, and in turn, it influences how individuals perceive and interact with society. Recognizing societal attitudes, norms, and expectations related to sexuality allows individuals to understand how these factors have influenced their own sexual self. This awareness can lead to questioning and challenging societal norms and advocating for sexual rights and equality.
  • Self-Acceptance and Self-Empowerment
    • - Embracing and accepting one's sexual self can be a powerful act of self-empowerment. Society often imposes shame, guilt, or stigmatization on various aspects of sexuality. Developing a positive and accepting attitude toward one's sexual self enables individuals to overcome these societal pressures and fosters self-love, self-esteem, and self-confidence.

Overall, the sexual self is an integral part of an individual's identity, contributing to their self-understanding, personal growth, emotional well-being, and relationships. Exploring, understanding, and accepting one's sexual self can lead to a more authentic and fulfilling life. It is important to note that the understanding of the sexual self is subjective and can vary across individuals based on their unique experiences, perspectives, and cultural backgrounds.





Tips and Advices for College Students



The following are characteristics of sexually healthy adults that can be a useful guide and some are our personal tips and advices for college students. However sexual health is developed over a life-span, from cradle to grave. Integrating sexuality into one’s life in a balanced way is a life-time endeavor.



Communication

  • Interact with all genders in appropriate and respectful ways.
  • Communicate effectively with family and friends.
  • If necessary, ask questions to adults about sexual issues.
  • Communicate respectfully if their desires to have sex and not to have sex.
  • Accept refusals of sex without hostility or feeling insulted.
  • Can physically express feelings of attraction and desire in ways that do not focus on the genitals (e.g holding, caressing, kissing, etc.)
  • Talk with a partner about sexual activity before it occurs, including limits, contraceptive and condom use, and meaning in the relationship.
  • Listen to and respect others' boundaries and limits.
  • Sensitive to non-verbal cues of others' boundaries and limits.


Relationships

  • Develop friendships that do not have a sexual agenda.
  • Avoid exploitative relationships.
  • Choose a partner that is responsible, trustworthy, safe and giving.
  • Can be sexually intimate without being physical (e.g talk about sexual feelings, verbally express attractions, do things that makes the partner arouse).
  • Can express themselves in ways other than genitally (e.g holding, caressing, kissing, and etc.)
  • Take personal responsibility for their own boundaries.


Self-Esteem & Self-Worth

  • Appreciates their own bodies.
  • Sensually aware and able to stay conscious in their bodies.
  • Can touch their own bodies without feeling shame or disgust.
  • Allow themselves to experience pleasurable sensual and sexual feelings.
  • Have the capacity to nurture themselves and others, and accepts nurturing from others.
  • Being happy in sexual experiences of their choosing.
  • Knows when they need to touch rather than to have sex, and tries to get their satisfactions and needs be fulfilled.
  • Have a developed sense of self, understands their ownself.
  • Enjoy sexual feelings without necessariliy acting upon them.
  • Comfortable with their sexual identity and orientation.
  • Aware of the impact of the negative sexual experiences such as sexual abuse, and the impact of cultural messages on their sexual development.
  • Taking steps to address issues that have arisen as a result of past experiences.
  • Feels confident in their ability to set appropriate boundaries.
  • Realizes that by working through sexual issues, individuals may heal psychologically and emotionally from the past traumatic experiences.


Education

  • Realizes the consequences of sexual activity.
  • Compherends the impact of media messages on thoughts, feelings, values, and behaviors related to sexuality.
  • Understands that the drive for sex is powerful and can be integrated into one's life in positive and healthy ways.
  • Respects the right of all people to enjoy and engage in the full range of consensual, non-exploitative sexual behaviors.


Values

  • Decides on what is personally "right" and act on these values.
  • Demonstrates tolerance for people with different values.
  • Not threatened by others with sexual orientation different from theirs.
  • Shows respect to others whose cultural values, ethnic heritage, age, socioeconomic status, religion, and gender that are different from theirs.


Contraception, Protection & Body Integrity

  • Takes responsibility for their own bodies and their own orgasms.
  • If sexually active, use contraception effectively to avoid unplanned pregnancy and use condoms for safer sex and avoids contracting or spreading a sexually transmitted disease.
  • Practices health-promoting behaviors, such as regular check-ups, breast or testicular selft-exams, regular and routine testing for STDs.
